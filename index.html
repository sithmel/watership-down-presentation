Understanding teams with

Watership Down

Maurizio Lupo

@sithmel

Hi, today I am going to talk about teams and teamwork.

...Introduce yourself...

A couple of years ago I was asked to manage my first team as principal engineer. After a short while I was helping out 2 teams dealing with different projects/areas.
Despite having 20 odd years of experience in engineering, I realised I didn't have much experience in leading people.
So I decided to do some reading.
My wife suggested me to read this book, she studied it at the University (she studied Psychology). It is about explaining many different models to "read" a team and understand its characteristics

Applying the model

And I found this model applies very well to Watership Down.
Watership Down is a fantasy adventure novel by English author Richard Adams, published in 1972.
The story features a small group of rabbits with their own culture, language, proverbs, poetry, and mythology.
The novel follows the rabbits as they escape the destruction of their warren and seek a place to establish a new home, encountering perils and temptations along the way.

What I am going to do now, is to pick up some episode from the book and show you many different lens we can use to observe a team.
Without further ado, let's see how we can read the story in this new light.

A new warren

This is how everything starts:
Fiver, a young rabbit, receives a frightening vision of his warren's imminent destruction. When he and his brother Hazel fail to convince their chief rabbit of the need to evacuate, they set out on their own, accompanied by nine other rabbits who choose to go with them.

A group of individuals is not a team.

They can become a team.

If they have a common GOAL

The goal should be:

  • discussed within the team
  • explicit rather than implicit
  • compatible with organisation goal
  • supported by the organisation
  • measurable
  • doable

The journey

This new group of rabbits sets on a journey to find a new home.
It is a dangerous situation: rabbits have numerous natural predators; foxes, weasel etc. Their survival strategy is to run and hide in their holes, but they can't because they are far from their old home.

But they are a group of resourceful rabbits.
Hazel realizes how difficult the situation is, and does his best to take care of the smallest and more vulnerable rabbits.
There is Bigwig, the strongest rabbit, that goes patrolling the area, to check if it is safe.
Dandelion helps conforting the rabbits, telling them stories.
Blackberry, the smartest rabbit, figures out a way to float the rabbits on a piece of wood, and to cross a river safely.

Roles cover these areas:

  • result
  • methodology
  • innovation
  • communication
Different roles are emerging in this group of rabbits.
Some member can cover more than one area.
In a team a person discover their real value, through the difference with the other members.
An unhealthy team tries to suppress that.

Issues around roles:

  • missing key areas
  • leader-does-all
  • member without role
Unhealthy teams miss one of them or let the leader to cover all

Hospitality

The rabbits go through several adventures before Hazel successfully brings them to a field where they believe they can live.
But the field is already inhabited by a group of rabbits, who seem strange but let the travelers stay with them.
Fiver warns the rabbits not to join the new warren, but they do not listen to him because the living is easy and there is food for everyone.
There is something odd about the warren, but they cannot figure out what it is.
Finally, after an argument with Fiver, Bigwig gets caught in a snare.
Fiver figures it all out, explaining to the group that a farmer leaves the food behind for the rabbits in order to fatten them up before he catches them in his snares. They decide to leave

Managing conflicts (some guidelines):

  • never ignore
  • do not blame but find causes
  • negotiate and not barter
  • talk facts and not opinions
  • value different point of view
  • value common points
  • do not put proposal in conflict, but in relation
Conflicts are a positive thing, a moment of growth in a team. But they need to be managed or otherwise they can escalate and create problems.

Watership Down

The rabbits travel on until they reach Watership Down. At the top there is a perfect field for a rabbit warren.
They settle down in the field, but then Hazel realizes that they need does (female rabbits) to mate with (and to dig holes), as they have only bucks and their warren will not last long without does.

Emerging team characteristics:


Openess: how neat are the boundaries of the team?

Cohesion: do we share the same norms and values ?

Interdependence: how much we depend each other?

Fragmentation: can we recognise subgroups in the team?

Specialisation: are we able to cover all necessary functions?

Integration: do we accept each other ideas?

So far we discussed the relation of team members; but teams can have a characteristic of their own

Kehaar

The rabbits befriend a wounded bird, Kehaar, and after he heals he searches for other warrens so they can get some does.

Do not use rabbit language with birds!

Take into consideration culture, roles, context

In communication, everything you say is interpreted. It is put in relation with the role of the speaker, and with the listener You need to take into consideration the participants and context. For example using jargon, technical words, idioms etc. Every team develop is own jargon but we need to be aware of it, otherwise it is going to isolate our team from the others. In an international team we need to consider how cultures influence the way we communicate. The role is also very important.

Efrafa

Kehar founds a big overcrowded warren a few miles of distance. Hazel decide to send an expedition.
The expedition comes back roughed up, describing Efrafa as a horrible warren run by a militaristic warlord.
Hazel decides that they must go to Efrafa and get some does, because otherwise they will not survive.
Hazel is attempting to do something extremely difficult, and he hopes to be able to do it by utilizing all of the rabbits' resources to the fullest.
Blackberry will provide the plan,
Bigwig the strength and daring,
Kehaar the element of surprise,
Fiver the guidance,
and Hazel the coordination and leadership.

Institutional leader

Responsible for team's output, goal and resources

The institutional leader, being appointed by the organisation, is responsible for the team output, and for the goals and resources.

Leadership issues

  • Lack of competence
  • Tyrant leader
  • Different from team expectations
The leader should be able to provide what is needed to the team, Failure to do so, and the group won't recognise the role. A strong leader can lead the team to a state of lack of self responsibility. From the member point of view, every failure is the leader fault, every success is because of them. The opposite for the leader.

Epilogue

Having a reference model to analyse a team is particularly useful.
Watership down offers a chance to apply that model and understand how a team can work together,
to achieve goals that are unthinkable for an individual.
Through those lens You will be able to easily recognise team and leadership issues and if possible helping to fix them.
